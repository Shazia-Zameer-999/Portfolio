I started programming because I was curious about how software worked under the hood. I stayed because I realized it's one of the few skills where you can build something real — something that runs, that other people use — from scratch, alone, with just a laptop and a problem worth solving.

I'm currently a BTech Computer Science student, and while I take academics seriously, most of what I've actually learned has come from building things: real freelance projects, open source contributions, and the kind of bugs that teach you more than any textbook chapter.

Right now I'm preparing for international internships — not just to get a stamp on my CV, but because I want to work on systems at a scale I can't manufacture on my own. I want to see how the best engineers think, and I want to be in the room where it happens.
